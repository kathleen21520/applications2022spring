Research: UI Conventions        

Nike (https://www.nike.com/): Women running shoes

  • What UI elements did I see and/or interact with while finding, comparing, selecting, and configuring the specific product?
  • there’s a megamenu, and the options change as you hover over different titles eg. “women” vs “men”
  • looking for women running shoes, hover over top banner “women” and found header of “shoes” and underneath is the subtitle of “running”
  • the process was not completely smooth because you have to ensure your mouse hovers exactly within the “women” box so that the dropdown menu doesn’t disappear
  • left sidebar: help narrow down to show options you want more specifically
  • when clicking on checkboxes, narrows down to show shoes that only meets the criteria
  • think “support level” criteria is good insight for comparing product
  • think “shoe feel” criteria is confusing to the consumer
  • highlights the specific things of the item, makes the product more outstanding
  • search bar: while typing the search query, is showing products that match more and changes actively
  • Have I seen this UI element before in other contexts?
  • yes, it’s common to see the image of the item and then caption of it underneath in grid form in vertical scroll
  • Did I know what this element would do before I interacted with it? Why?
  • most of the time, yes, because I’ve been onto a lot of websites like these (learned from experience)
  • sometimes would need to hover to completely know if the text is clickable or would be surprised when something expands,
  • for example, hovering over the description under a product, the gray subtitles disappear and small boxes of images appear underneath the main image that shows variety in the product (eg. color)
  • What general problem is this element designed to handle?
  • some of the buttons are not fluent in their appearance (the top banner of the website)
  • it could be inconvenient in my opinion that there is no limit set to the end of the vertical scroll, I prefer a certain length of the vertical scroll and have the option of clicking to the next pages
  • Why was it used in this context? Is it successful?
  • the most useful and unique element is the search bar, where suggested products appear and attempt to guess what you are searching for as you type. I think it is successful because Nike has a lot of products and especially with the “unlimited” vertical scroll it could be difficult to find what I want immediately.

Casetify (https://www.casetify.com/): iPhone 12 case

  • What UI elements did I see and/or interact with while finding, comparing, selecting, and configuring the specific product?
  • there is a megamenu on the top, followed by a big slide deck of images that slides automatically to introduce the products visually. more vertical scroll down is more images categorized with some themes. a quick way to introduce different sections that they want to highlight.
  • when clicked into one of the options in the megamenu, eg. iPhone 12, there is a sidebar with checkboxes that can filter products that fit the criteria, with phone case designs shown in grid form + vertical scroll.
  • Have I seen this UI element before in other contexts?
  • the homepage structure design is very similar to Nike’s. megamenu + visual impact as the main focus.
  • the product page design is also very similar to Nike’s.
  • Did I know what this element would do before I interacted with it? Why?
  • yes, it is a common homepage + product page design.
  • What general problem is this element designed to handle?
  • this homepage design is effective in quickly introducing what products they have without requiring users to read much text. it is way more interesting to look at the products themselves rather than their description at first glance.
  • the product page design is also the best way to present the products to the customers as well.
  • Why was it used in this context? Is it successful?
  • like Nike, there are a big variety of products in which visual impact would allow the users to have a bigger picture. it is successful in this context because users who usually go to Casetify are people who care a lot about visuals and customizations to their own product.

Chanel (https://www.chanel.com/us/): Handbag

  • What UI elements did I see and/or interact with while finding, comparing, selecting, and configuring the specific product?
  • the menu on top is not a megamenu. it is simple in categorization (no subcategories). edit: some categories would expand and take up the whole screen into a submenu, some would directly take you to the product page.
  • images below the menu are all bleeding across the website: they act as ‘backgrounds’ with a title towards the bottom of each image.
  • Have I seen this UI element before in other contexts?
  • the menu having this much variety is rare to me, but I can definitely follow because it is easy to understand.
  • Did I know what this element would do before I interacted with it? Why?
  • I had to play around a bit because the previous two would expand the menu when hovering over each category, but for Chanel, you need to click on the category to see what happens.
  • What general problem is this element designed to handle?
  • I think this solves the problem of accidentally hovering over a part when you didn’t mean it. This happened to the two previous web pages and could end up eye-soring and busy because there are just too many options that you get overwhelmed. Since Chanel is in comparison a more sophisticated and not mass-produced product brand, it makes sense that the UI conventions are more conserved.
  • Why was it used in this context? Is it successful?
  • I think the homepage being simple but at the same time sophisticated (eg. when vertical scrolling the images move along a bit) is successful because it feels well-controlled. every section filling up the whole screen also makes it feel like a presentation and emphasizes each individual product/section way better than the previous two web pages. the photographs also give off a magazine feel which makes the products feel more luxurious.

Apple (https://www.apple.com/mac/): Macbook Pro

  • What UI elements did I see and/or interact with while finding, comparing, selecting, and configuring the specific product?
  • the menu does not have any submenu at all. it is clearer than Chanel with the UI convention of when hovering over the text changes color, which is a cue for users to click onto it.
  • the homepage also includes big bleeding images across the screen acting as ‘backgrounds’ like Chanel, but gives more importance to the product’s description as they’re placed towards the top of each image.
  • when clicked onto “Mac”, the product page has an interesting ‘second’ menu below the original one seen on homepage. it has image icons of each Mac type which makes it very clear for users to understand what types of Mac there are. Below there is a chart that compares different Mac sizes and its attributes with some visual icons as well. then below there are many side info that is related to Mac which is helpful (eg. Mac accessories).
  • Have I seen this UI element before in other contexts?
  • I cannot recall any other webpages that also have this. I think what Apple has especially in the product pages are very creative.
  • Did I know what this element would do before I interacted with it? Why?
  • Even though it is very unique, it is not difficult to understand at all because there are many icons and the info are all very organized and concise.
  • What general problem is this element designed to handle?
  • The elements on the product page are designed so that users can very easily compare the products and decide what they want without having to read body descriptions for each individually.
  • Why was it used in this context? Is it successful?
  • I think the product page is super successful because I have experience in needing to open each product individually and compare by myself. The way that Apple does it allows me to compare the products side-by-side which are very convenient.

Summarizing my Findings

  • What elements or patterns seem universal?
  • the homepage having a menu on the top followed by images of the products seemed like a formula for the homepage to feel like one.
  • the search bar and shopping bag are always on the top right corner.
  • Did I see anything that felt different or unusual?
  • not really anything that popped out, but would definitely argue that Apple’s product page stood out from the rest in its unique info presentment.
  • What elements or patterns were specific to the store or product?
  • Nike’s smart search bar trying to guess what users want to find is like the in-person experience of a shop assistant trying to find the pair of shoes that you are looking for.
  • Chanel’s design in its webpage overall of extreme simplicity in mainly only photographs and little to no text feels like flipping through a fashion magazine.
  • Apple’s product icons and diagrams create the impression that Apple is very detail-oriented and take pride in their products.
  • Were there any patterns or techniques that made one store stand out? Could the other stores have applied that technique successfully?
  • I think Chanel’s webpage stood out the most to me if I had to pick one out of the four because of its successful imitation of a magazine feel. It is interesting how it becomes a hybrid of a physical magazine and digital platform. I think Nike and Casetify would not suit this technique because they should continue to exhibit the grand collection of products they offer. Apple’s webpage would not really want this either because they would want to focus on purely the products and would not need models. Most importantly, the extreme lack of further attributes would go against Apple doing a superb job in organizing detailed info.
  • Finally, how did you feel when interacting with these sites? When were you happiest/most comfortable? When were you disrupted/confused/bothered?
  • Overall, there are no big surprises or confusion in how the webpages navigate. I was the happiest when exploring Chanel because of the aesthetics and also Apple because of how well presented the visuals are with the needed info.
  • If there was some dissatisfaction, it would be for Nike and Casetify that the products are less organized despite the sidebars. The users are expected to know the big picture and actively categorize what they want which kind of means that they would have to already know what product they are searching for beforehand. Otherwise, they would have to explore and click through all categories one by one.
